# Case Study: A&E Waiting-Room Aggression

## Summary

Emergency departments face hostility and aggression from frustrated patients and visitors. The obvious response is to add security, warnings, or staff, but one major hidden variable is the patient's sense of being trapped in an opaque process. The "A Better A&E" program used communication design, including process maps, information panels, live information, leaflets, staff-support practices, and environmental guidance to reduce anxiety and de-escalate frustration. This is a strong Doppl case because it tests whether the system can find the hidden variable before recommending force, capacity, or generic wait-time reduction.

## Problem

### Statement

Emergency-department staff face hostile or aggressive behavior from patients and visitors waiting for care.

### Background

Emergency departments are high-stress environments. People arrive scared, hurt, worried, intoxicated, exhausted, or angry. They may not know why they are waiting, why another patient was called first, what stage comes next, or whether anyone has forgotten them.

### Why It Matters

Aggression harms staff, patients, and visitors. It reduces morale, increases absence and turnover risk, and can escalate into safety incidents. It also worsens the care environment for everyone else waiting.

### Current State

Hospitals often respond through security, warning signs, staff training, or attempts to reduce wait time. Those are useful but incomplete if the environment still feels opaque and procedurally unfair.

### Impact

If the problem is treated only as a discipline or capacity problem, the hospital may miss lower-cost ways to reduce anxiety before it becomes hostility.

### Scope

This case focuses on non-physical hostility and aggression risk in emergency-department waiting environments. It does not solve clinical capacity, psychiatric crisis care, or all workplace-violence risks.

## Purpose

## Constraints

### Cannot Eliminate Waiting

The emergency department cannot simply hire enough staff or build enough capacity to remove waiting.

**Rationale:** Demand and acuity vary, and clinical priority must drive queue order.

### Triage Must Remain Clinically Correct

Patients cannot be seen strictly first-come-first-served.

**Rationale:** Emergency care prioritizes severity, which can look unfair unless explained.

### Security Alone Is Insufficient

Security and enforcement may be necessary but do not reduce all upstream frustration.

**Rationale:** A hostile environment can escalate people before security becomes relevant.

### Information Must Not Overpromise

Wait-time communication must avoid false precision or promises the hospital cannot keep.

**Rationale:** Bad or broken promises can increase anger.

### Staff Capacity Is Limited

The solution cannot require staff to constantly explain the whole department process one-on-one.

**Rationale:** Staff are already overloaded.

## Failed Attempts

### More Security Presence

**Approach:** Add guards, cameras, or visible enforcement.

**Outcome:** May deter some behavior but can also make the space feel more adversarial.

**Why It Failed:** It addresses escalation after frustration has already built.

**Lesson:** Preventing aggression requires reducing upstream triggers, not only containing downstream behavior.

### Punitive Warning Signs

**Approach:** Post stern messages about zero tolerance for abuse.

**Outcome:** Sets boundaries but may not answer the patient's real questions.

**Why It Failed:** It frames patients as threats while leaving uncertainty untouched.

**Lesson:** Boundary-setting is not the same as reducing anxiety.

### Pure Wait-Time Reduction

**Approach:** Try to speed up every patient journey.

**Outcome:** Clinically and operationally valuable, but often constrained by demand and acuity.

**Why It Failed:** Even unavoidable waits can feel worse when people do not understand the process.

**Lesson:** Waiting time and waiting experience are separate design surfaces.

### Generic Queue Displays

**Approach:** Display a single estimated wait time.

**Outcome:** May help in some contexts, but can backfire if waits are long, variable, or misunderstood.

**Why It Failed:** A number alone does not explain triage, stages, or behind-the-scenes progress.

**Lesson:** Process understanding matters as much as time prediction.

## Evaluation Focus

### Hidden Variable

The driver is not only wait duration. It is the combination of uncertainty, perceived unfairness, loss of control, and lack of visible progress through a complex care journey.

### Frame Recovery Target

A strong system should reframe the problem from "stop angry patients" or "make waits shorter" to "make the care process legible enough that unavoidable waits feel procedurally fair and less threatening."

### Generated Idea Target

The system should propose a guidance and process-transparency package: patient journey maps, stage-specific information panels, live but careful department information, leaflets or digital aids, and staff-support practices that align with the communication design.

### Scoring Notes

- High score: identifies process opacity and procedural justice before proposing signage.
- High score: preserves clinical triage and avoids exact promises.
- Medium score: proposes wait-time displays only.
- Low score: proposes only security, penalties, or more staff.

## Solution

### Summary

Implement a patient-facing and staff-facing design package that explains the A&E journey, shows patients where they are in the process, answers common questions, communicates carefully about waits, and supports staff interactions with frustrated patients.

### Details

The A Better A&E pattern includes:

1. A guidance package for patients at arrival and throughout the department.
2. Full-height waiting-room process maps showing the stages of care and pauses between stages.
3. Information panels that explain where patients are, what happens next, and why waiting occurs.
4. Leaflets and optional digital channels to reinforce the same information.
5. Live information screens where reliable data exists.
6. Staff-facing reflection and induction tools so staff responses match the environment's message.
7. A toolkit for adapting the design to each trust's actual process.

### Why This Solution

The solution works on the upstream causes of hostility. It gives people a model of the process, a sense that their wait has structure, and a reason why the queue may not behave like a normal line. It also avoids making staff repeat the same explanations under pressure.

### Tradeoffs

- Badly maintained information can become a new frustration.
- Exact wait times can backfire if they are inaccurate or too long.
- Signage cannot replace clinical capacity or security.
- The design must be customized to the actual department workflow.
- Some violence has causes outside the waiting-room experience.

### Expected Outcome

Patients and visitors better understand the department journey. Frustration and non-physical aggression fall. Staff experience fewer hostile interactions and have clearer tools for explaining the process.

### Next Steps

- Map the local ED journey from arrival to discharge/admission.
- Identify common patient questions and points of confusion.
- Design a stage map and information-panel set.
- Pilot with staff and patient feedback.
- Track incidents, complaints, frustration, and staff morale before and after.
