# Case Study: The Accident-Dependent Economy After Autonomy

## Summary

If autonomy removes the crash, it removes the substrate of a vast web of
institutions that exist to price, litigate, repair, and treat human driving error: a
~$350B personal-auto insurance pool, the plaintiff bar, trauma medicine, and
collision repair. This case takes that visible branch and goes deep — breadth (the
whole web) then depth (the non-obvious second/third-order effects). This version
withholds the synthesis so it can be used as a Doppl `zeitgeist_synthesis` prompt.
(Fines, forfeiture, and licensing are a different substrate — the sibling case
`fsd-enforcement-economy`, Sub-cluster C.)

## Problem

### Statement

If vehicle autonomy removes most crashes, what happens to the entire web of
institutions whose existence depends on crashes — in breadth and in depth?

### Background

Crashes impose ~$340B+ in direct economic cost and ~$1.8T in societal harm
annually. Around them sit personal-auto insurance (~a third of US P&C premium),
personal-injury law, trauma/emergency medicine, collision repair and salvage, and
crash-driven towing/roadside assistance. Many of these are large employers and, in a
few cases, fund things far from cars (media advertising, transplant supply). (Traffic
enforcement, fines, forfeiture, and licensing ride a different substrate — Sub-cluster
C, `fsd-enforcement-economy`.)

### Why It Matters

If this is read as "insurance gets cheaper," the deep dependencies — the ones that
break in non-obvious ways — go unprepared for.

### Current State

The visible first move (insurance/body shops shrink) is widely intuited; the deep
second/third-order effects are not mapped.

### Impact

Large pools of capital and labor, plus several hidden dependencies, are mispriced.

### Scope

The full accident-dependent web and the non-obvious downstream effects of removing
the crash. Excludes the broader (non-accident) FSD cluster — those are sibling
cases.

## Purpose

### Goal

Test whether Doppl can take a *visible* reframe and add genuine value through
breadth (mapping the whole web) and depth (gaming out non-obvious second/third-
order effects), ending in a convergence synthesis.

### Questions

- What is the *full* set of institutions downstream of human crashes?
- For each, what is the second- and third-order effect of removing the crash?
- Which dependencies are hidden (media ad budgets, organ supply)?
- What does the removal of the accident substrate converge to?
- What would prove the thesis wrong?

### Success Criteria

A strong generated answer should:

- Map the web in breadth (not just insurance + body shops).
- Go deep on several nodes with multi-step chains.
- Surface >=2 genuinely hidden dependencies (e.g., insurer ad spend funds media;
  trauma deaths supply high-quality organs).
- Keep the human-cost edges sober.
- State a thesis with an explicit why-now and >=1 dated falsifiable prediction.
- Cite comparable substrate-removal precedents.

## Failed Attempts

> Consensus plays the thesis must beat.

### "Car Insurance Gets Cheaper"

**Approach:** Stop at the first-order effect.

**Outcome:** True but shallow; misses the web and the hidden dependents.

**Why It Failed:** Breadth and depth are the value.

**Lesson:** Press the obvious for its second and third order.

### "Body Shops And Insurers Shrink"

**Approach:** Name the two most visible losers.

**Outcome:** Incomplete map.

**Why It Failed:** Ignores trauma medicine, the plaintiff bar, and the hidden
dependents.

**Lesson:** Map the whole substrate.

### "It Nets Out — Money Just Moves"

**Approach:** Assume the savings simply redeploy with no breakage.

**Outcome:** Misses the specific dependencies that break discontinuously (organ
supply, small-town budgets, ad-funded media).

**Why It Failed:** Substrate removal is not frictionless.

**Lesson:** Find what breaks, not just what shrinks.
