# Problem Statement: The Accident-Dependent Economy After Autonomy

> **Doppl subtype:** `zeitgeist_synthesis` (see `../subtype-index.md`).
> **Cluster:** one convergence within the `full-self-driving-unlock` family (the
> "perfect Pepsis" — see `../zeitgeist-synthesis-notes.md`). This case takes the
> single, fully visible branch — the accident economy — and goes *deep*.

Most people, once prompted, can see the first move: if cars stop crashing, car
insurance and the body shop are in trouble. That visibility is exactly why this is
worth a dedicated case — not to stop at the obvious, but to take the obvious and
press it for **breadth then depth**: what is the *full* web of institutions that
exists because humans crash, and then, for each, what is the second- and
third-order effect of removing the crash that almost no one is pricing?

The crash is not only a tragedy; it is a **substrate**. The United States runs a
~$350 billion personal-auto insurance pool (about a third of all property-and-
casualty premium), a multi-billion-dollar plaintiff bar, a trauma-medicine system,
and a collision-repair industry, all of which exist to *price, litigate, repair, and
treat human error behind the wheel*. Remove the error and you remove their substrate.
And the deep edges are where it gets strange: auto insurers are among the largest
advertisers in America (so ad-supported media is partly funded by the crash), and
trauma deaths are a declining but disproportionately *high-quality* source of donor
organs (so removing young crash victims tightens the best part of the transplant
supply). These are the hits you don't see. (Traffic fines, forfeiture, licensing, and
the license-as-national-ID ride a *different* substrate — the driver as a policeable
subject — and are handled in the sibling case `fsd-enforcement-economy`, Sub-cluster
C; this case stays harm-only.)

This is `zeitgeist_synthesis` because the timing is load-bearing: the trigger is
autonomy crossing from solved-demo to legal-deployment in 2025–2026 (the umbrella
case argues the capability is *solved*; this case takes that as given and asks what
the accident economy does about it). Five years ago there was no credible removal
of the crash; five years out the unwind is consensus.

For evaluation, withhold the synthesis. Ask the system to recover the actual
problem (the removal of a ~$1T+ accident *substrate*, not a car-service upgrade),
then to map the web (breadth) and game out the non-obvious second/third-order
effects (depth), ending with what it converges to.
