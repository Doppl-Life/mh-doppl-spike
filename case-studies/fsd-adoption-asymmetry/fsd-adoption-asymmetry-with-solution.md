# Case Study: The Unevenly Distributed Future (Evaluator)

## Summary

Autonomy arrives as a geofenced patchwork, so its story is the *asymmetry*, not the
average. Three gulfs open at once: a perception split (a minority lives in the
autonomous world; a majority doesn't believe it's real), an intra-national
divergence (served vs. lagging metros, like the broadband divide with capital and
talent following), and a first/third-world split with a two-sided leapfrog wildcard
(mobile/M-Pesa pattern). The non-obvious consequences are the asymmetries
themselves — arbitrage and migration to served zones, mispricing by the
disbelievers ("the punch you don't see coming"), and boundary effects between served
and unserved jurisdictions. This file carries the evaluator targets; never show it
during generation.

## Evaluation Focus

### Stated Problem Or Symptom

"Autonomy is coming" (a uniform average arrival).

### Actual Problem

How to read and position for a *patchwork* regime change whose consequences are the
asymmetries it creates — perception, intra-national, and international (with leapfrog
potential) — rather than an evenly distributed arrival.

### Deleted Assumptions

- That arrival is uniform / a wave.
- That the average ("it's coming") captures the effect.
- That concentration is one-way (ignoring leapfrog).

### Hidden Variable

The deployment mechanism *is* the thesis: geofencing + mapping + state-by-state
regulation + weather + politics force a patchwork, so a widening gulf — experiential,
economic, political, international — is the structural consequence. The disbelievers'
perception lag is itself an exploitable asymmetry (the punch they don't see coming).

### Frame Recovery Target

Reframe average → asymmetry; map the three gulfs; treat leapfrog two-sidedly;
identify arbitrage/migration and the blindsided; recognize the rollout-window timing.

### Generated Idea Target

A `ZeitgeistSynthesisPayload` with a branched asymmetry map + synthesis, thesis,
audience, cited signals, why-now, >=1 dated falsifiable prediction, prior art.

## Solution

### Summary

Autonomy is a patchwork, so the story is the gulf it opens. Map three asymmetries —
perception (believers vs. disbelievers), intra-national (served vs. lagging metros),
and international (rich-metro concentration vs. a leapfrog wildcard) — and the
arbitrage, migration, and blindsiding they produce. The future is already here for
some; the value and the fault lines are in how unevenly it's distributed.

### Details

`ZeitgeistSynthesisPayload`:

- **thesis:** Autonomy arrives as a geofenced patchwork, not a wave, so its dominant
  near-term effect is a *widening asymmetry* — a perception gulf (a minority lives in
  it; a majority doesn't believe it's real), an intra-national gulf (served metros
  pull ahead on safety/cost/reclaimed time while laggards stall), and a
  first/third-world gulf (with a two-sided leapfrog wildcard) — and the people not
  paying attention are about to be hit by a regime change already real for others.
- **audience:** Macro/geography strategists, policymakers, real-estate and
  emerging-market investors.
- **currentSignals:**
  - AV services are geofenced and hyper-local, launched metro-by-metro and gated by
    mapping, state-by-state regulation, weather, and politics; deployment sits in a
    handful of US metros and TX/CA leads on legality (Signal Set D4).
  - A perception split is observable: most people have never ridden an AV and many
    doubt it works, while a minority use it routinely (adoption-curve / lived
    experience).
  - International concentration in rich-country metros, with Waymo eyeing
    London/Tokyo — alongside the mobile/M-Pesa precedent that adoption can invert in
    specific emerging markets.
- **whyNow:** The gulf is opening *now*, during city-by-city rollout. In 2020 there
  was nothing to distribute (uniform absence); by ~2035 coverage has either converged
  or permanently bifurcated. An "unevenness" thesis only exists in this window.
- **gamedOutAsymmetryMap:**
  - *Branch A — perception gulf:* believers (who don't touch the wheel) vs.
    disbelievers (who won't update until it's on top of them) → the disbelievers
    (including investors, workers, policymakers) misprice the change → those who see
    it early reposition (the arbitrage *is* the perception gap). "We've already been
    hit, and most haven't noticed."
  - *Branch B — intra-national divergence:* served metros get safer, cheaper,
    time-reclaimed; laggards don't → like the broadband divide, talent, capital, and
    real estate follow coverage → wage/real-estate divergence and a served-vs-unserved
    political/cultural split. *Boundary effect (ties the cluster):* the accident
    economy unwinds in served counties first, creating cross-border insurance /
    litigation / ER arbitrage between adjacent served and unserved jurisdictions.
  - *Branch C — first/third-world + leapfrog wildcard:* concentration in rich-country
    metros widens global gaps in road safety, productivity, and logistics cost →
    *but* a motivated operator in a light-legacy, light-regulation emerging market
    could leapfrog (the mobile/M-Pesa inversion), deploying faster than incumbents
    expect. Game both directions; do not assume one-way.
  - *Branch D — "it won't hit everywhere, but it will":* rural/low-density and
    poorly-mapped areas lag; dense mapped metros lead → a patchwork that advances
    unevenly for years, not a clean national switch.
- **synthesis (what it converges to):** The regime change is a patchwork, and the
  asymmetry is the story. The future arrives at full force for some while others
  don't believe it exists; the gulf — experiential, economic, political,
  international — widens before it converges, minting winners among those who see it
  and reposition and blindsiding those who don't. Among the FSD cluster, this is the
  branch that governs *how* and *for whom* all the other branches land.
- **falsifiablePredictions:**
  1. By 2030, AV-served population is concentrated in a small number of metros,
     producing a measurable double-digit gap between leading and lagging large US
     metros.
  2. By 2030, >=1 measurable arbitrage/migration or real-estate signal tied to AV
     availability (people/businesses relocating to served metros, or a coverage-zone
     real-estate premium).
  3. By 2032, either a measurable first/third-world divergence (AV-served population
     far higher in high-income metros) or a documented emerging-market leapfrog
     deployment.
  4. [Perception] Through 2030, surveys continue to show a large majority have never
     used an AV even as served-metro usage becomes routine — a persistent
     belief/experience gap.
- **comparablePriorArt:** The digital divide (broadband adoption asymmetry creating
  durable economic gaps); mobile phones leapfrogging landlines and M-Pesa leapfrogging
  banks in Kenya (the two-sided wildcard); rural electrification (urban-first
  rollout); COVID remote-work splitting knowledge from physical workers. Used to
  stress-test, not as the argument.

### Why This Solution

It fits the recovered problem (a patchwork whose story is the asymmetry) rather than
the stated symptom (a uniform arrival), is grounded in deployment geography and
distribution precedents, falsifiable, timing-specific, and two-sided on the
international angle.

### Tradeoffs

- Convergence could be faster than expected, narrowing the window.
- The leapfrog wildcard is genuinely uncertain in both magnitude and location.
- Divergence framing must stay sober (no triumphalism about places left behind).

### Expected Outcome

If correct, those who read the asymmetry early reposition (geography, real estate,
EM bets) ahead of the disbelievers.

### Next Steps

- Track served-population concentration, coverage-zone real-estate premia, AV-usage
  perception surveys, and any emerging-market leapfrog launches.
