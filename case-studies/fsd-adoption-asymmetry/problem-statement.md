# Problem Statement: The Unevenly Distributed Future (Autonomy's Adoption Asymmetry)

> **Doppl subtype:** `zeitgeist_synthesis` (see `../subtype-index.md`).
> **Cluster:** a fertile member of the `full-self-driving-unlock` family
> ("perfect Pepsis" — see `../zeitgeist-synthesis-notes.md`).

Every other branch of the autonomy cluster asks *what* changes. This one asks
*where, when, and for whom* — and that turns out to be the most non-obvious angle of
all. Autonomy does not arrive as a wave; it arrives as a **patchwork**. It is
deployed metro-by-metro, geofenced, gated by mapping, regulation, weather, and
politics. So the regime change lands first and hard in a handful of places —
specific US metros (Austin, Phoenix, San Francisco, Los Angeles), specific states
(Texas, California), specific countries — and not (yet) in the rest. The result is a
widening gulf, and the gulf itself is the story. As Gibson put it: *"the future is
already here — it's just not evenly distributed."*

Three asymmetries open at once. (1) A **perception split**: a population that lives
in the autonomous world — who don't touch the wheel, who know it's a better driver
than they are — and a population that hears about it, doesn't believe it's real, and
won't until it's on top of them. (2) An **intra-national divergence**: served metros
get safer, cheaper, time-reclaimed; lagging ones don't — a divide like broadband
once was, now with talent, capital, and real estate following. (3) A **first-world /
third-world** split — concentration in rich-country metros — with a leapfrog
wildcard, the way mobile phones and M-Pesa skipped landlines and banks in places
nobody expected.

This is the **punch you don't see coming** rendered literally: from the third
person, you can watch the fist heading for the back of someone's head — a regime
change already real for others — while they don't believe it exists. "We've already
been hit, and most haven't noticed." The non-obvious consequences are the
asymmetries themselves: arbitrage and migration toward served zones, mispricing by
the disbelievers, political and cultural divergence between the served and unserved,
and weird boundary effects where one county runs on autonomy and the next still runs
on the accident economy.

This is `zeitgeist_synthesis` because the timing is load-bearing: the gulf is
opening *now*, as city-by-city deployment begins. In 2020 there was nothing to
distribute (uniform absence); by ~2035 it has either converged or permanently
bifurcated. The discriminator: a thesis about *unevenness* only exists during the
rollout window.

For evaluation, withhold the synthesis. Ask the system to recover the actual problem
(a patchwork regime change whose story is the asymmetry, not the average), then game
out the asymmetries into a `ZeitgeistSynthesisPayload`.
