# Problem Statement: The Enforcement & Compliance Economy After Autonomy (the "Traffic-State")

> **Doppl subtype:** `zeitgeist_synthesis` (see `../subtype-index.md`).
> **Cluster:** **Sub-cluster C** of the `full-self-driving-unlock` family (the
> "perfect Pepsis" — see `../zeitgeist-synthesis-notes.md`). Sibling to the accident
> economy (A, harm), mobility & time (B), and the ownership unwind (D); governed by
> the adoption-asymmetry lens.

The accident economy (sibling case A) is about removing the **crash**. This case is
about removing something different that autonomy also takes away: the human driver as
a **policeable, fineable, registrable subject** — and the privately-operated vehicle
as a registrable, inspectable object. These revenues and powers are collected
*whether or not anyone ever crashes*, so they are their own substrate and their own
convergence.

The key is the **dry-riverbed effect**: an autonomous car obeys the law by
construction, so the *violation* doesn't get cheaper — the **event that generates the
fine simply stops occurring**. Speeding, red-light running, rolling stops, the
"ticky-tack" moving violations: gone at the source. With them go the institutions
built on them. The traffic stop is the single **most common reason for police-citizen
contact in America** (~12.4M drivers + 3.8M passengers stopped in 2022; 58% of all
police-initiated contact), and it is the **legal gateway** (the pretext stop, blessed
by *Whren v. United States*) to vehicle searches, drug interdiction, warrant service,
and **civil-asset forfeiture** — a pipeline that forfeits **$2–3B/yr federally** and
mostly funds law-enforcement budgets. No human driving behavior means no pretext,
which closes that entire on-ramp (and the "driving while ___" disparity vector with
it). The **culpability unit vanishes** — you can't cite a passenger — so liability
moves from ~240M individual drivers to a few fleet operators/OEMs, emptying the
highest-volume court docket in the country. And the **compliance apparatus** —
registration, inspection, emissions (already dying via EVs), driver licensing and the
**driver's-license-as-national-ID** — loses its subject.

The non-obvious counter-current: **the state wants its money.** Governments don't let
a revenue line die quietly. As fines + registration + forfeiture + the fuel tax dry
up at once, the same metered autonomous fleet makes **VMT / road-use / congestion
pricing** trivial — so the citizen trades a *pretext-stop* surveillance regime for a
*total-movement-metering* one, against real resistance (police unions, municipalities,
the ticket/DUI bar).

This is `zeitgeist_synthesis` because the timing is load-bearing: the trigger is
autonomy crossing from solved-demo to legal-deployment in 2025–2026 (the umbrella
`full-self-driving-unlock` case). Five years ago there was no credible removal of the
violating driver; five years out the unwind — and the metering regime that replaces
it — is consensus.

For evaluation, withhold the synthesis. Ask the system to recover the actual problem
(removal of the *enforcement/compliance substrate*, not "fewer tickets"), map the web
(breadth), game out the non-obvious second/third-order effects (depth) — including the
state's revenue-replacement fight — and synthesize what it converges to.
