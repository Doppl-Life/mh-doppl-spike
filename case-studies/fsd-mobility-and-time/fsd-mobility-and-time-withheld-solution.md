# Case Study: Autonomy Re-Prices Mobility & Time (the Driver/Friction Unlock)

## Summary

Autonomy's non-obvious impact is not "robotaxis in cities" — it is one convergence
that fires when you remove the driver and the friction of distance, re-pricing
**time and distance** across three chapters of a single story: (1) door-to-door
travel collapses in the 200–700-mile band (where short-haul flying is already weak);
(2) ~3M driving jobs and a $175–200B roadside economy unwind while ~1 hr/day/person
of commute time is reclaimed; (3) geography re-prices as sleeper-commuting welds
megaregions. This version withholds the synthesis for use as a Doppl
`zeitgeist_synthesis` prompt.

## Problem

### Statement

Autonomy is arriving. What does it do to mobility and time — travel, the people who
drive for a living, the hour spent driving, and where people live?

### Background

Three facts sit side by side, usually discussed separately. (1) Short-haul flights
(<500 mi) are ~half of US domestic flights but only ~30% of passengers, declining for
a decade; cars already dominate 100–500-mile trips and air crosses over only ~700 mi;
ground wins under ~3 hours door-to-door. (2) ~3 million Americans drive for a living
(≈2.07M heavy/tractor-trailer + ≈1M light/delivery; BLS 2024), served by a $175–200B
travel-center industry (~4,000–6,500 locations) whose sites are often the largest
employer/taxpayer in town; the average one-way commute is 27.2 min (≈1 hr/day),
69.2% driving alone. (3) Where people can live is capped by daily drive time.

### Why It Matters

If autonomy is read as "robotaxis in cities" — or these three facts as unrelated
trends — the larger convergence (a re-pricing of time and distance across aviation,
labor, hospitality, freight, attention, and geography) goes unpriced.

### Current State

The conversation is urban robotaxis; the intercity substitution, the driver/roadside
unwind, the reclaimed hour, and the geography re-pricing are examined in isolation if
at all.

### Impact

Mispricing the convergence leaves regional aviation, small airports, the roadside
economy, driver-wage-dependent local tax bases, hospitality, and secondary-city real
estate unprepared.

### Scope

The mobility-&-time effects of cheap, sleepable, driver-free movement: intercity
(200–700 mi) door-to-door travel; driving labor + the reclaimed commute hour;
geography/real estate. This is **Sub-cluster B** of the FSD cluster. Excludes pure
urban ride-hail and the **accident economy** (Sub-cluster A — a sibling story on a
different substrate, the crash).

## Failed Attempts

> Consensus plays the thesis must beat.

### "Robotaxis In Cities"

**Approach:** Keep autonomy's impact urban.

**Outcome:** Misses the intercity substitution and the labor/time/geography re-pricing.

**Why It Failed:** The 200–700-mile band and the driving-labor base are the bigger,
weaker targets.

**Lesson:** Go intercity and follow the driver/friction removal.

### "Three Unrelated Trends"

**Approach:** Treat flight decline, the trucker shortage, and housing prices as
separate stories.

**Outcome:** Misses the one substrate that connects them.

**Why It Failed:** Removing the driver and the cost of distance fires all three at
once.

**Lesson:** Synthesize the chapters; find the shared substrate.

### "Flying Is Always Faster"

**Approach:** Compare gate-to-gate flight time.

**Outcome:** Ignores the four-hour door-to-door reality.

**Why It Failed:** Door-to-door is the metric that decides modal share.

**Lesson:** Count the whole journey.

### "People Won't Sleep In Cars"

**Approach:** Assume autonomy is just hands-off driving.

**Outcome:** Misses the sleeper-pod reconfiguration that unlocks overnight range.

**Why It Failed:** Removing the driver removes the fatigue ceiling.

**Lesson:** The vehicle becomes a room.
