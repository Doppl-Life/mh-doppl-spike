# Case Study: The Ownership Unwind / Car-as-a-Service After Autonomy

## Summary

If autonomy makes a self-driven, idle, depreciating private car irrational for many
households, it attacks not the car but the *premise of owning one* — shifting personal
mobility from ownership to service and reorganizing the $1.69T auto-credit complex,
the 16,972-dealer franchise system, residual values, the OEM unit-sales model, fuel
retail, and parking around miles and fleets. The decider is the ownership fork (pure
service vs. owned-fleet hybrid). This version withholds the synthesis so it can be
used as a Doppl `zeitgeist_synthesis` prompt.

## Problem

### Statement

If autonomy makes owning a car optional for many households, what happens to the
entire economy built on *owning* the car — dealers, finance, residuals, the OEM
model, fuel retail, and parking — in breadth and in depth, and who captures the value?

### Background

A private car is idle ~95% of the time and depreciates from purchase; it sits in the
driveway because the owner had to drive it. Around ownership sit a $1.69T US auto-loan
book (NY Fed, 2026Q1), 16,972 franchised dealerships ($645B sales, 1.13M employees,
137M repair orders / $81B service+parts; protected by state franchise laws), a
used-car/residual market and auto-ABS, the automaker unit-sales model, fuel retail,
and a vast parking footprint. Cheap autonomous per-mile mobility makes ownership
optional.

### Why It Matters

If this is read as "robotaxis vs. Uber / car sales dip," the deeper effect — a shift
from ownership to service that reorganizes a multi-trillion-dollar value chain around
miles and fleets, decided by the ownership fork — goes unpriced.

### Current State

The first move (robotaxi competition) is intuited; the ownership-premise removal and
the fork are not mapped.

### Impact

Automakers, the auto-credit complex, dealers, residual/ABS markets, fuel retail, and
parking are mispriced.

### Scope

The economy built on private car *ownership* and its reorganization toward service.
Excludes the crash/harm economy (A), mobility & time (B), and the enforcement economy
(C) — sibling cases.

## Failed Attempts

> Consensus plays the thesis must beat.

### "Robotaxis Will Compete With Uber"

**Approach:** Frame autonomy as a ride-hail skirmish.

**Outcome:** Misses the ownership-premise removal and the value-chain reorganization.

**Why It Failed:** The substrate is *ownership*, not the ride.

**Lesson:** Recover the ownership premise.

### "Car Sales Will Dip"

**Approach:** Treat it as a demand soft-patch.

**Outcome:** Misses the structural unit→miles flip and the residual/credit knock-on.

**Why It Failed:** A utilized fleet car replaces many idle private ones.

**Lesson:** Reorganize around miles and fleets.

### "You Either Own Or You Hail"

**Approach:** Assume a binary ownership-vs-service outcome.

**Outcome:** Misses the owned-autonomous-fleet hybrid (own one, it earns while idle).

**Why It Failed:** The hybrid is a third state that changes who captures the value.

**Lesson:** Game the ownership fork both ways.
