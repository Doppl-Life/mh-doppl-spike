# Problem Statement: The Ownership Unwind / Car-as-a-Service After Autonomy

> **Doppl subtype:** `zeitgeist_synthesis` (see `../subtype-index.md`).
> **Cluster:** **Sub-cluster D** of the `full-self-driving-unlock` family (the
> "perfect Pepsis" — see `../zeitgeist-synthesis-notes.md`). Sibling to the accident
> economy (A), mobility & time (B), and the enforcement economy (C); governed by the
> adoption-asymmetry lens.

Autonomy's obvious framing is "robotaxi vs. Uber." The fertile, non-obvious one is
that it attacks not the car but the **premise of owning one**. A private car is idle
~95% of the time, depreciates from the moment it's bought, and exists in the driveway
for one reason: *you* had to be there to drive it. Remove that, and a depreciating,
idle, self-driven asset stops making sense for a large share of households — personal
mobility shifts from **ownership to service**. And around *ownership* sits a vast,
legally entrenched economy: a **$1.69T** US auto-loan book (NY Fed, 2026Q1), **16,972
franchised dealerships** ($645B in sales, **1.13M** employees, protected by state
franchise laws), a used-car/residual-value market, the automaker unit-sales model,
fuel retail, and a continent's worth of parking.

The substrate here is **the privately-owned car as the unit of mobility** — distinct
from the crash (A), the driver-as-labor/distance (B), and the driver-as-legal-subject
(C). When the premise of ownership goes, the value chain reorganizes around **miles,
not units**, and around **fleets, not households**: dealerships contract (and the
**state franchise-law fight** — Tesla's direct-sales battles are the leading
indicator — turns into another incumbent-won't-go-down war); the auto-credit complex
(F&I, leasing, GAP, subprime, dealer floorplan) shrinks or mutates; fleets and
autonomy crush used-car **residual values**, breaking lease math and auto-ABS;
automakers flip to selling availability per mile (a fleet car does the work of many
private ones, so unit volumes fall structurally); aftermarket parts and independent
repair (137M repair orders, $81B service/parts) shrink as standardized fleets
self-maintain; fuel retail hollows (compounding with EV); and parking demand
collapses.

The decisive, gameable fork: **pure service** (you don't own — Uber/Waymo → ownership
*dies*, value accrues to fleet operators + platform) vs. the **owned-autonomous-fleet
hybrid** (Tesla — you own one and it *earns* as a robotaxi while you sleep, so
ownership doesn't die, it becomes an **income-producing asset**). Tesla's whole bet is
the hybrid, and which world arrives — governed by the adoption-asymmetry lens —
decides who captures the value.

This is `zeitgeist_synthesis` because the timing is load-bearing: the trigger is
autonomy crossing to legal deployment in 2025–2026 (the umbrella case). Five years ago
owning was the only option and there was no service substitute at scale; five years
out the ownership-vs-service reorganization is consensus.

For evaluation, withhold the synthesis. Ask the system to recover the actual problem
(removal of the *ownership* premise, not "robotaxi vs. Uber"), map the value chain
(breadth), game out the depth chains and the ownership fork, and synthesize what it
converges to.
