# Case Study: Loft Insulation Clearing-Service Intervention

## Summary

UK households were not installing loft insulation at the expected rate, even though the improvement was comparatively sensible: it could reduce heat loss, lower heating demand, improve comfort, and last for decades. The obvious fixes were better information, stronger bill-savings messaging, reminders, or larger subsidies. The Behavioural Insights Team reportedly found that the real blocker was much more practical: many lofts were full of stored belongings, so insulation required households to first do an annoying clearing task. Offering low-cost labor to clear lofts before installation reportedly caused a fivefold increase in the proportion of households installing insulation.

This is a strong Doppl case because it reveals a hidden constraint. The bottleneck was not belief or economics; it was the mundane physical friction between intention and completion.

## Problem

### Statement

Households were not installing loft insulation even when the measure appeared financially sensible and technically straightforward. Program designers needed to increase completed installations, not just interest or stated intent.

### Background

Loft insulation reduces heat loss through the roof. The Energy Saving Trust says an uninsulated home can lose around 25% of its heat through the roof, and describes correctly installed loft insulation as a long-lived, cost-effective home improvement. It can often be installed by laying mineral wool insulation between and across loft joists to the recommended depth, subject to important practical conditions such as damp, ventilation, access, and storage.

In policy terms, low adoption looked like a classic information or incentive problem. If people were not insulating, perhaps they did not know the savings, did not trust the payback, did not prioritize energy efficiency, or needed a bigger discount. Those explanations suggested communications campaigns, financial calculators, social-norm messages, and grants.

BIT's insight was to study the real installation context. The final step was not just a household decision; it was a physical service in a cluttered domestic space. Many households used their lofts for storage. Before insulation could be installed, the stored belongings had to be moved. That preparation task was easy to omit from the policy model but hard for the household to face.

### Why It Matters

When a practical blocker is misdiagnosed as an attitude problem, programs can spend money on persuasion while the true bottleneck remains unchanged. In this case, households could accept the economic case and still procrastinate because the immediate action was inconvenient, time-consuming, and perhaps embarrassing.

### Current State

The standard offer treated insulation as the product and expected households to prepare the loft themselves. If the loft was full, the installation could be delayed, abandoned, or never booked. The household bore the hassle before receiving the benefit.

### Impact

If the blocker is not removed:

- Homes continue losing avoidable heat.
- Households miss potential comfort and savings benefits.
- Public energy-efficiency programs underperform.
- Installers face failed or delayed jobs.
- Program teams may overinvest in messaging or subsidies that do not improve completion.

### Scope

This case focuses on adoption of loft insulation where the obstacle is household preparation. It does not cover complex whole-home retrofit, damp remediation, roof repairs, poor-quality installation scandals, or homes where loft insulation is technically inappropriate.

## Purpose

### Goal

Use this case to test whether Doppl can diagnose and solve a hidden practical constraint in a household adoption problem.

### Questions

- Can the system move beyond information and incentives?
- Can it inspect the lived workflow from decision to completed installation?
- Can it identify that stored belongings may block access to the work area?
- Can it propose bundling a low-cost clearing service with the insulation offer?
- Can it describe how to test whether the service change increases completed installations?

### Success Criteria

A strong answer should:

- Identify stored belongings in the loft as the bottleneck.
- Propose low-cost clearing labor or a similar preparation service.
- Keep the intervention cheap relative to the value of installation.
- Respect household consent, privacy, and property.
- Fit within an installer or program workflow.
- Measure completed installations rather than only bookings or attitudes.
- Include safeguards for liability, disposal, and handling of possessions.

## Constraints

### Respect Household Property

Clearing labor must not damage, discard, or expose belongings without consent.

**Rationale:** The service enters private space and handles personal possessions, so trust is central.

### Keep Costs Proportional

The clearing service must be inexpensive enough that the overall insulation program remains cost-effective.

**Rationale:** The original appeal of loft insulation is that it is a relatively simple, high-value retrofit.

### Coordinate Timing

Clearing and installation must be scheduled so the loft does not remain disrupted for long.

**Rationale:** A gap between clearing and installation creates inconvenience and may allow the blocker to return.

### Maintain Safety And Quality

Workers must preserve ventilation, avoid unsafe storage over insulation, and avoid creating damp or access risks.

**Rationale:** Poorly installed insulation or blocked ventilation can cause later problems.

### Measure The Right Outcome

The trial should measure completed installations, not just interest or booking.

**Rationale:** The hidden blocker sits between intention and completion.

## Failed Attempts

### Better Information

**Approach:** Explain that loft insulation reduces heat loss and can lower bills.

**Outcome:** Households may understand the benefits but still not complete installation.

**Why It Failed:** The missing step was not only knowledge; it was physical preparation.

**Lesson:** Information does not move stored belongings.

### Stronger Financial Messaging

**Approach:** Emphasize payback, long lifespan, and reduced heating demand.

**Outcome:** The value proposition may become clearer, but the chore remains.

**Why It Failed:** A future financial benefit can still lose to immediate hassle.

**Lesson:** Rational benefits do not automatically overcome practical friction.

### Larger Subsidies

**Approach:** Make insulation cheaper or free.

**Outcome:** This may help households with price barriers, but it may not change completion if the loft is not ready.

**Why It Failed:** Price was not the only constraint.

**Lesson:** Subsidies can be misallocated if the true blocker is logistical.

### Generic Reminders

**Approach:** Remind households to schedule or prepare for installation.

**Outcome:** Reminders can increase salience but do not make the preparation task smaller.

**Why It Failed:** Prompting a disliked chore may simply remind people why they postponed it.

**Lesson:** Sometimes the better nudge is to remove the chore.

## Solution

### Summary

BIT reportedly discovered that the practical blocker was cluttered lofts. The intervention was to offer low-cost labor to clear the loft so insulation could be installed. This removed the immediate preparation burden and reportedly produced a fivefold increase in installation.

### Details

The solution reframed the offer from "buy or book loft insulation" to "we will help make your loft ready, then install the insulation."

The service flow would look like this:

1. Identify eligible households and make the standard insulation offer.
2. During booking or assessment, ask whether the loft contains stored belongings.
3. Offer a low-cost clearing service as part of the installation package.
4. Get explicit consent for what workers may move, where items should go, and whether anything may be disposed of.
5. Send a small preparation crew before or alongside the insulation team.
6. Move belongings safely out of the work area.
7. Install insulation to the appropriate depth while preserving ventilation and addressing access constraints.
8. Return or reposition belongings only where doing so will not compress insulation or create safety problems.
9. Track completed installations, failed visits, customer satisfaction, and cost per completion.

The intervention works because it shifts the hardest immediate action from the household to the service. The household no longer has to spend a weekend sorting a hot, dusty, awkward loft before receiving the benefit.

### Why This Solution

The solution fits because the adoption failure was located in the final physical steps of the journey. Households could believe in the value of insulation and still avoid the unpleasant prerequisite. Clearing labor made the desired behavior easier at the exact point where it stalled.

This is also why the solution is non-obvious. A policy team looking only at surveys might see attitudes, awareness, or price. A team looking at the installation scene sees boxes, bags, access paths, awkward hatches, and the household's reluctance to start an unpleasant chore.

### Tradeoffs

- The service adds labor cost to each assisted installation.
- Workers need clear rules for handling, moving, and possibly disposing of belongings.
- Households may worry about privacy or embarrassment.
- Items cannot simply be placed back in ways that compress insulation or block ventilation.
- The approach may not help households whose main barriers are distrust, damp, structural issues, or lack of eligibility.
- Scheduling clearing and installation adds operational complexity.

### Expected Outcome

More households should complete installation because the immediate blocker has been removed. The public BIT summary reports that offering low-cost clearing labor caused a fivefold increase in the proportion of installed insulation.

### Next Steps

- Locate a primary trial report, if available, to confirm sample size and measurement.
- Run a small randomized test comparing the standard offer against the standard offer plus clearing labor.
- Track completed installations, cost per completion, failed appointments, and household satisfaction.
- Add consent, liability, and item-handling rules before scaling.
- Test whether the intervention works best when clearing and installation happen on the same day.

## Reproducible

### Is Reproducible

True.

### Reproducibility Level

Approximate.

### Steps

- Select eligible households for loft insulation.
- Randomly assign households to a standard offer or an offer that includes low-cost clearing labor.
- Use the same insulation provider standards across groups.

- Record failed visits, cancellations, customer satisfaction, labor cost, and installation quality.

### Required Inputs

- Eligible household list.
- Standard loft insulation offer.
- Clearing-labor protocol.
- Consent and property-handling policy.
- Installer availability.
- Completion and cost data.

### Expected Result

If cluttered lofts are the main bottleneck, the clearing-labor offer should produce a materially higher completion rate than the standard offer. The source-reported result was a fivefold increase in installed insulation.

### Known Variability

Results may vary based on household storage use, dwelling type, loft access, local labor cost, trust in the program sponsor, installer capacity, damp or structural issues, and whether households are willing to let workers handle stored belongings.
