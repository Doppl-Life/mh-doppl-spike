# Original Source Material

This file consolidates the original raw source material that several public case studies in this folder were derived from. It exists so the folder is self-contained: case files reference the relevant list/item below instead of pointing at an external attachment path.

These are candidate "triage" lists — collections of problem sketches gathered for evaluation. Each derived case study extracts a single item into the full case-study schema. Content below is preserved verbatim from the original capture; only section headers were added.

## Candidate Triage List A — Behavioral Reframes (numbered)

> Derived cases that draw from this list: Airport Liquid Congestion (item 4), London Underground Map Distortion (item 7), A&E Waiting-Room Aggression (item 11).

1. The Red Bull Antithesis. Source: Rory Sutherland, Alchemy / TED 2009 (TED Blog). Problem sketch: You want to launch a new soft drink to compete with Coca-Cola. Traditional economics says it needs to taste better, cost less, and come in a larger container to provide value. Constraints: You cannot out-spend Coke on traditional distribution or match their exact flavor profile. Solution status: KNOWN. Hint: Make it taste aggressively clinical, sell it in a tiny, expensive can, and hint that it's slightly dangerous. Why it's a candidate: Pure behavioral reframe—violating every logical rule of product design to create a new category. Tags: marketing, behavioral, perception.

2. The EasyJet Tarmac Transmutation. Source: Rory Sutherland, The Knowledge Project (Transcript). Problem sketch: An EasyJet flight lands, but all terminal gates are full. Passengers must be dumped onto the cold tarmac and loaded onto a cramped shuttle bus, an experience universally hated and rated as "second-class service." Constraints: You cannot build new gates, and physics mandates the bus must be used. Solution status: KNOWN. Hint: Frame the bus not as a demotion, but as an exclusive shortcut past 700 yards of terminal walking directly to passport control. Why it's a candidate: A cost-free, linguistic choice-architecture fix for a physical bottleneck. Tags: logistics, behavioral, customer-experience.

3. The Eurostar Speed vs. Champagne Paradox. Source: Rory Sutherland, TEDGlobal 2009 (TED Blog). Problem sketch: Engineers want to spend £6 billion to build new tracks to shave 40 minutes off the London-to-Paris Eurostar train journey to improve passenger satisfaction. Constraints: Building new high-speed rail tracks takes decades, destroys environments, and costs billions. Solution status: KNOWN. Hint: Spend a tiny fraction of the money on onboard WiFi and supermodels serving free luxury champagne. Why it's a candidate: Realizing that "pleasurable time spent" scales better than "physical speed engineered." Tags: physics, engineering, behavioral, logic-vs-magic.

4. The Airport Security Fluid Smuggling Bottleneck. Source: Rory Sutherland, The Knowledge Project (Transcript). Problem sketch: Over 50% of airport security backlogs are caused by normal passengers accidentally leaving 105ml toothpaste tubes or water bottles in their bags. It slows down throughput, penalizing the airport's core metric. Constraints: You cannot change the TSA/aviation laws regarding liquids, and you can't force passengers to read signs. Solution status: OPEN. Hint: Requires a psychological intervention before the gray bins that alters memory or changes compliance incentives. Why it's a candidate: A massive logistical friction point caused entirely by human absentmindedness rather than malice. Tags: security, logistics, behavioral.

5. The Jeep Wrangler "Round Eyes" Reframe. Source: Clotaire Rapaille, The Culture Code (Illuminz Review). Problem sketch: In the 1990s, Chrysler wanted to update the Jeep Wrangler to better compete with luxury SUVs. Focus groups explicitly demanded a more comfortable ride, leather seats, and square, modern headlights. When Chrysler gave them exactly what they asked for, sales tanked. Constraints: The consumer's conscious requests directly contradicted their unconscious buying triggers. Solution status: KNOWN. Hint: Strip out the luxury, revert the headlights to primitive circles, and market the vehicle not as a car, but as a "horse." Why it's a candidate: Exposes the classic market research failure: people don't do what they say. Tags: marketing, behavioral, anthropology.

6. The Japanese Coffee Imprint Crisis. Source: Clotaire Rapaille, The Culture Code (Illuminz Review). Problem sketch: In the 1970s, Nestlé tried to introduce instant coffee to Japan. They spent heavily on advertising and execution, but sales were completely flat because the Japanese consumer had zero emotional connection or childhood memory associated with coffee—their entire culture was anchored in tea. Constraints: You cannot force adults to switch lifelong habits overnight using standard logical value propositions. Solution status: KNOWN. Hint: Ignore the adults entirely; instead, sell coffee-flavored desserts and candies to children to play a 20-year long-game. Why it's a candidate: A massive multi-decade strategic reframe that prioritized psychological imprinting over instant revenue. Tags: marketing, behavioral, strategic-timeline.

7. The London Underground Map Distortions. Source: Rory Sutherland, The Waiter's Pad / Ogilvy Data Case Studies (PDF). Problem sketch: Commuters overcrowding certain London Underground lines cause severe platform delays. The physical infrastructure cannot handle more trains. Constraints: You cannot dig new tunnels or change the physical geography of the city tracks. Solution status: KNOWN. Hint: Manipulate the scale of the iconic schematic tube map to make alternative, under-utilized routes look shorter and more central than they actually are. Why it's a candidate: Exploits the fact that humans navigate using the map representation rather than physical reality. Tags: logistics, behavioral, spatial-design.

8. The Anti-Fly Irrationality Signal. Source: Rory Sutherland, Thinking like Rory Sutherland (PDF). Problem sketch: To prevent flies from entering food-prep areas, standard practice is to use chemical sprays or noisy electric bug zappers, which look ugly and alarm customers. Constraints: Must be completely safe for human food prep, clean, and whisper-quiet. Solution status: KNOWN. Hint: Hang clear bags of water with a shiny coin inside near the entrance to scramble the fly's compound eyes. Why it's a candidate: Uses a biological exploit in the target's sensory system rather than brute-force elimination. Tags: physics, biology, hospitality.

9. The GPS "Distance Remaining" Psychological Shift. Source: Rory Sutherland, Alchemy / Various Interviews. Problem sketch: Early GPS systems and taxi rides made passengers highly anxious because they could see the car stuck in traffic but had no transparent view of when the torment would end. Constraints: You cannot eliminate traffic jams or change city street layouts. Solution status: KNOWN. Hint: Do not focus on making the car go faster; focus on displaying a dynamic, highly visible countdown timer. Why it's a candidate: Proves that the uncertainty of delay causes far more psychological pain than the delay itself. Tags: behavioral, software-architecture, human-factors.

10. The 45° Shreddies Geometry Pivot. Source: Rory Sutherland, Alchemy / Ogilvy Canada Campaign. Problem sketch: A legacy whole-wheat square cereal (Shreddies) has flatlined in sales. The product is fundamentally unchangeable due to manufacturing plant tooling, and there is no budget for new ingredients or flavor formulations. Constraints: Zero product formula changes allowed; factory machinery cannot be re-engineered. Solution status: KNOWN. Hint: Keep the cereal exactly the same but tell consumers you rotated the squares by 45 degrees to create a brand-new "diamond" experience. Why it's a candidate: The absolute ultimate pure-play example of generating massive economic value entirely through cognitive reframing. Tags: marketing, behavioral, manufacturing.

11. The Emergency Room "Wait-Time" Aggression Bottleneck. Source: Choice Architecture Case Studies / Behavioral Insights Team (UK). Problem sketch: Hospital emergency rooms experience high rates of verbal abuse and physical aggression directed at staff by frustrated patients who have been waiting hours to be seen. Constraints: You cannot hire more doctors or magically speed up triage processing times. Solution status: KNOWN. Hint: Install a highly specific, linear map showing patients exactly where they are in the multi-stage triage process, eliminating the "black box" mystery. Why it's a candidate: Resolves an operational security risk entirely through transparent, low-cost information design. Tags: healthcare, behavioral, security.

12. The Drone Swarm Target-Saturation Open Problem. Source: Defense Technology Triage Reports / Unverified Tech War Stories. Problem sketch: Modern military bases or critical infrastructure are highly vulnerable to low-cost, coordinated drone swarms. Standard kinetic defense (missiles, machine guns) runs out of ammunition quickly and costs millions per shot. Electronic jamming can be countered by autonomous, pre-programmed waypoints. Constraints: Cannot use expensive interceptors; cannot jam civilian frequencies nearby; must handle 50+ simultaneous targets. Solution status: OPEN. Why it's a candidate: A classic asymmetric attrition problem where the attacker's cost is pennies compared to the defender's dollars. Tags: engineering, security, physics, OPEN.

13. The 401k Impulse-Saving Deficit. Source: Richard Thaler / Rory Sutherland TED Talk (TED Blog). Problem sketch: Modern digital commerce has completely optimized for friction-free impulse buying (1-Click checkout). However, human psychology defaults to immediate gratification, making impulse saving non-existent for the average worker. Constraints: Cannot legally force people to lock away their cash outside of standard tax-advantaged payroll structures. Solution status: OPEN. Hint: Requires an interface intervention that makes saving feel as instantly rewarding or addictive as buying a physical object. Why it's a candidate: A structural flaw in human choice architecture that could be resolved with a clever interface or psychological loop. Tags: fintech, behavioral, software-architecture, OPEN.

14. The "Slow Elevator" Mirror Illusion. Source: Classic Lateral Thinking Lore / Sutherland Reference. Problem sketch: Post-WWII office tower tenants heavily complain that the newly installed automated elevators take too long to arrive, threatening to break their leases. Constraints: Re-engineering the physical cable systems or elevator shafts is structurally impossible or cost-prohibitive. Solution status: KNOWN. Hint: Give people a highly absorbing, self-critical task to do while standing in the lobby, like looking at themselves. Why it's a candidate: Changes the psychological perception of time rather than trying to alter physical reality. Tags: architecture, engineering, behavioral.

The Houston Baggage Walk Reframe. The operational evaluation of terminal passenger flows is documented by Stephen Shapiro in his analysis of wait-time optimization. The primary administrative problem involved intense customer dissatisfaction regarding long luggage wait times at the terminal, which persisted even after hiring additional baggage handlers to reduce the objective wait to eight minutes. The underlying operational bottleneck was not chronological delay, but the cognitive friction of passive, unproductive waiting. Under severe physical constraints, the airport could not accelerate mechanical baggage conveyor systems any further. The solution status is classified as KNOWN, utilizing a physical rerouting of arrival gates to artificially elongate the walk time and eliminate passive standing at the baggage carousel. This case serves as a prime candidate for triage because it illustrates how altering pacing and shifting from passive waiting to active movement can neutralize customer friction without physical infrastructure expansion. Taxonomy tags for this case include logistics, aviation, behavioral, and perception.

The Swedish Speed Camera Lottery. The behavioral dynamics of civic traffic compliance are recorded in the documented history of the Volkswagen Fun Theory campaign in Stockholm, Sweden. The primary problem was that traditional speed enforcement rely entirely on punitive, negative reinforcement, which triggers resentment, avoidance behaviors, and temporary compliance only in the immediate vicinity of radar units. Under typical urban constraints, municipalities cannot afford to pay positive cash rewards to millions of law-abiding drivers, and strict national privacy laws prohibit using official police enforcement cameras to process non-offenders. The solution status is classified as KNOWN, employing a system where a percentage of fines collected from speeders is automatically pooled into a lottery draw for drivers who comply with the posted limit. This case is an excellent candidate due to its self-funding positive reinforcement loop, which successfully reduced average vehicle speeds in school zones by 22%. Taxonomy tags for this case include civic, traffic, gamification, behavioral, and legal.

The solution status is KNOWN, relying on a counterintuitive mandate that shifted the social meaning of the garment rather than outlawing it. This case stands as a premier candidate because it showcases how an aggressive cultural policy can be successfully executed without coercion by using a psychological reframe that leverages social stigma rather than legal force.
