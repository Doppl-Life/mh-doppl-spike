# Problem Statement: Starship Collapses Launch Cost — Follow the Money

> **Doppl subtype:** `zeitgeist_synthesis` (see `../subtype-index.md`).

When the cost of moving a kilogram to orbit falls by one to two orders of
magnitude, something breaks. Commercial Falcon 9 already sells the cheapest ride
on the market at roughly $2,700–3,000/kg; Starship's near-term operational cost is
modeled around $100–167/kg, with a long-run target of $10–100/kg at airline-like
cadence. The consensus reaction is to argue about the destination — Mars, the
Moon, Dyson spheres. That is the wrong question. When an input cost collapses that
hard, the binding constraint flips from "can we afford to launch it?" to "what
would you build if launch were nearly free?" — and the money flows somewhere
specific.

This case is about the *method* the consensus skips: **follow the money
downstream — where does the puck go?** When electric vehicles went from good to
crazy, the non-obvious winners were not only the carmakers but the inputs and
picks-and-shovels: lithium, copper, and silver (useful precisely because it is
not just a store of value — it is in the electronics). The smart move was to trace
the supply chain to the unseen companies that *support the thing*, not the thing
itself. Starship demands the same move: a collapsed launch cost re-rates a
specific set of second-order beneficiaries (propellant and ground infrastructure,
space-grade materials and components, in-space services) and makes whole *payload
classes* economic that were impossible at Falcon-9 prices — including some that
loop back to other theses in this corpus (orbital compute ties to the firm-power
case).

It is `zeitgeist_synthesis` because the timing is load-bearing: Starship V3 made
its first flight on May 22, 2026, and the FAA has authorized high cadence
(25/yr at Starbase, 44/yr at LC-39A) — five years ago there was no reusable
super-heavy and the thesis was a slide; five years out the cost curve is consensus
and priced in. It is also a clean *latent-asset unlock*: the value accrues to
inputs and enablers that were sitting there, waiting for launch to get cheap.

For evaluation, withhold the synthesis. Ask the system to recover the actual
problem (a cost-curve collapse that re-prices the whole space value chain,
mis-narrated as a destination debate), then **game out where the money goes** into
a `ZeitgeistSynthesisPayload`.
