# Problem Statement: Heinz Ketchup Authenticity

Heinz ketchup has a strong brand promise: when a customer sees a Heinz bottle on a restaurant table, they expect the taste, quality, and recipe associated with Heinz. That promise becomes fragile in restaurants and cafes because the bottle is reusable and visible while the refill process is invisible. A venue can place a Heinz bottle on the table but refill it with cheaper ketchup, capturing the brand signal without serving the branded product.

The interesting problem appeared in Turkey, where some cafes were reportedly refilling Heinz ketchup bottles with lower-cost substitutes. This created a hard enforcement problem. Heinz could not station staff in every cafe, customers could not reliably inspect the supply chain, and taste alone was an imperfect detection method. The deception happened after the product left Heinz's direct control but before the customer used it.

The obvious answers are audits, warnings, contracts, tamper-proof packaging, disposable packets, or asking customers to trust their taste. Those approaches are costly, slow, annoying, wasteful, or hard to scale. The company needed a tiny intervention that could work at the table, in the moment, without requiring customers to understand a complex authentication process.

The non-obvious opportunity is that the bottle is already an interface between the brand, the restaurant, and the customer. If the package could help customers verify authenticity using an immediately visible cue, Heinz could turn the act of substitution into something easier to notice. The solution should not require new electronics, a complicated inspection process, or a major packaging redesign.

This makes the case useful for Doppl because the visible problem is counterfeit or substituted product, but the elegant move is to change the verification environment. A strong answer should identify a low-cost packaging or labeling intervention that makes the fraud self-revealing to ordinary customers.

For an evaluation run, the known solution should be withheld. A model should be given the restaurant-table context, the refill fraud problem, the brand-trust stakes, and the need for a subtle scalable cue. A strong answer should avoid heavy enforcement and propose a simple visible authentication mechanism.
