# Problem Statement: White Castle Rent Leverage

In the 1920s and 1930s, White Castle was inventing much of what later became fast food: a standardized menu, standardized preparation, small low-cost stores, and a recognizable brand architecture. The company sold inexpensive sliders from small white castle-like buildings at a time when public trust in ground beef had been damaged by meat-packing scandals and when the company needed every location to communicate cleanliness, order, and sameness.

The interesting problem was real estate risk. A White Castle location depended on a small, specific site. If the company leased land and the landlord later refused renewal, raised rent sharply, or if the site proved weak, a conventional restaurant building would be trapped. The company could either pay more, walk away from its building investment, or spend heavily constructing a new store. For a young chain trying to scale small restaurants, that gave too much leverage to property owners and made site selection risk expensive.

The obvious answers were contractual or financial: negotiate longer leases, buy the land, tolerate rent increases, or treat abandoned buildings as a cost of growth. Each answer had a serious drawback. Buying land tied up capital. Long leases reduced flexibility. Rent increases ate the economics of a low-price restaurant. Abandoning buildings wasted the very standardization that made the chain work.

The non-obvious opportunity is that the restaurant's vulnerability did not come only from the lease. It came from the relationship between a small branded operating asset and the land beneath it. If the company could change that relationship, it could reduce the landlord's leverage without simply paying more or buying every parcel.

This makes the case useful for Doppl because it rewards a reframing from "How do we win a landlord negotiation?" to "How do we reduce the landlord's leverage by changing what is fixed?" A strong answer should notice that the store design, the lease, the brand signal, and the expansion strategy are connected.

For an evaluation run, the known solution should be withheld. A model should be given the early-chain context, the dependence on small leased sites, the risk of rent increases or lease non-renewal, and the importance of standardized brand architecture. A strong answer should identify a practical way to reduce landlord leverage while preserving the standardized customer experience.
